Concept Summary          Chapter 1

From a cosmological perspective our fragile Earth is a mere grain of sand whirling through space with another grain of sand, our Sun, at a great speed unnoticed by most of its inhabitants. Although some can be as large as half our solar system, billions of other grains of sand (stars) exist just within our galaxy — a single galaxy of an estimated 100 billion or more.  Plus, recent efforts to explain the origin of our universe and all its parts point to the possibility that our universe may be a mere bubble in an infinite sea of universes.

Our universe is one of endless variety, infinite microscopic textures, and vast cosmic distances where colossal processes are the norm within, scientists believe, a strange explosion (the Big Bang) of energy, matter, space, and time that began from a lonely spaceless point located "nowhere" approximately 14 billion years ago. Strange as these thoughts seem, scientists can describe "how" the universe is here rather than not here. They cannot, however, describe "why" it is here or why there is something rather than nothing.

Cosmologically speaking, our existence and that of our entire species seem insignificant, but cosmologically speaking we are the cosmologists. Although a species for only a relatively short period of time, we have been able to figure out some of the details of our cosmic home. We participate now in an endless detective story with every success at understanding producing a new mystery. This too is a mystery. Why should we know all that we know? Why should we be capable of such strange thoughts that work?

The Earthly platform from which we seek understanding is shared with an endless variety of biological brethren. Like us, they are precious new leaves at the end of a long evolutionary bush. They do not seem to be as smart as we are; they do not seem to be aware of atoms, photons, galaxies, supernovas, neutron stars, supernovas, black holes, and quasars. Nor do they know how to make biological, nuclear, and robotic weapons, and for all we know, it may be smart not to be too smart. Our intelligence has brought us far, but it has also brought us to the brink of total destruction. It cuts both ways, its application sometimes terrifies us, but it also reveals a humbling, sobering perspective of our cosmological home. Unlike most of our biological brethren, our survival is threatened most by our own actions, especially those that stem from an ignorant view of our true place in the universe. An awareness of a cosmological perspective is an essential step in encouraging the value choices to enable a successful future for our species.  This awareness helps us be aware of our true self-interest and that the flourishing of those we care about the most depends on human flourishing in general and global justice and sustainability.

Some key factual data to help with the big picture:

Speed of light = 186,000 miles per second
Light from the Sun to the Earth = about 8 minutes
Light from our Sun to the nearest star = about 4 years
Distance of the Sun to center of our galaxy = about 30,000 light years
Distance of the diameter of our galaxy = about 80,000 light years
Distance from our galaxy to the Andromeda galaxy = over 2 million light years
Number of galaxies in our universe = over 100 billion

If the age of the Universe was compressed into one calendar year:

Origin of the Universe -- January 1st
Origin of the Earth -- Mid-September
Dinosaurs -- December 25th-28th
Modern Humans -- last few minutes, December 31st.
