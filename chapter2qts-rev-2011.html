In 1981 the news media descended upon Tucson Arizona to interview a small religious sect that was waiting for Jesus Christ to take them to heaven. Bill Maupin, the leader of the Lighthouse Gospel Tract Foundation, had predicted that on June 28th the world would end, and all those who were to be saved by God would be "spirited aloft like helium balloons." About fifty people had gathered to experience the realization of the vision of their leader -- a vision, according to Maupin, resulting from 16 years of careful Biblical study and meditative prayer. With Maupin were an owner of an ornamental ironworks business, a doctor, a surgical technician, a painting contractor, and other members of suburbia who had quit their jobs, sold their homes and cars, all waiting for the fulfillment of what they called "rapture day."

On the 28th, except for an electrical storm, nothing happened. The news media left, but a few months later a follow up story appeared. Maupin admitted that obviously he had got the date wrong. He wanted to make it clear though that God was not to blame, their faith in Jesus was still strong, and that, "some day" they were "going up." All of the members agreed, it was their mistake, not God's.

A few years later a news item gave another account of a vision, this time involving a bold religious fund-raising appeal by evangelist Oral Roberts. Roberts said that Jesus had appeared to him and told him that he had been chosen to find a cure for cancer. This "supernatural breakthrough" would involve asking thousands of "prayer partners" to send $240 each, so that Roberts's Tower of Faith Research Center could be completed. Cures could then be found for cancer, and "other dread diseases," which are the "work of the devil."

In 1997 a group of about 30 people from the Heaven's Gate religious and business organization (they had a lucrative business in producing some of the first Web pages) committed suicide, believing that the comet Hale-Bopp approaching our Sun was being followed by a large space ship of extraterrestrial advanced beings. They believed that the beings were going to pick them up and take them to the next level of reality.

In 2011, a popular radio minister, Harold Camping, predicted that the world would end on May 21, 2011. Because Camping's ministry was broadcast on over 60 radio stations worldwide, the proliferation of social networking and Internet communication, plus millions of dollars spent on advertising, which included 2,000 billboards all over the United States, thousands of people were convinced this prediction was true. Camping had claimed that he calculated the date from numerous statements in the Bible. Many people quit their jobs and some charged up a huge credit card debt to take their families to special places for the end. When nothing happened, one spokesperson for Camping admitted that they were "disappointed" that 200 million people (Camping's estimate) were not transported to heaven and the rest of the billions of people on Earth did not suffer earthquake and other horrendous calamities as the Earth was destroyed. Another follower argued that the prayers must have worked, because God delayed the judgment day so that more people could be saved! Camping soon explained that the date should have been October 21, 2011, but that he was right about the May date being important. Instead of the "rapture day," this date was the "invisible (spiritual) judgment day," the day that God decided who would go to heaven. No word was given as to why God would need to wait five months to implement His decision. Camping's prediction produced some very harmful consequences. There were reports of suicides all over the world and one woman tried to kill her children because she did not want them to suffer during the apocalypse.

In this chapter we need to discuss the nature of evidence, and what having evidence for a belief means. From a scientific point of view we cannot claim categorically to know that the predictions of Maupin, Roberts, the Heaven's Gate group, and Camping are false. It is possible that tomorrow Maupin's group will experience their rapture day, Roberts or his followers will find a cure for cancer some day, the Heaven's Gate people are at another level of reality feeling sorry for us, and there was an invisible judgment day on May 21, 2011 that we will some day see the results of.

These beliefs are very strange. The Big Bang theory and many current scientific beliefs are also strange, uncomfortable, and seem to violate common sense. In general, most scientific beliefs are more incredible than religious ideas. There are beliefs about black holes, where time and space disappear and the end of time occurs in an instant; the concept of a "singularity," where, in the case of the origin of the Universe, all matter, space and time explode from a single point with no physical dimensions; quantum spaces and vacuums where nothingness consists of virtual, potential existence; the implications of relativity theory, where it is possible for a mother to take a long space voyage and become younger than her son; and perhaps the most bizarre of all, quantum objects, where the mathematical description of the energy of a single electron reveals that it can be in more than one place at a time, and even "tunnel" through space, popping in and out of existence. Why should we believe these beliefs are more reliable than the visions of Maupin, Roberts, and the Heaven's Gate group?

Many beliefs might be true, but to enter the arena of science a belief must be testable. Thus, first and foremost, what separates scientific theories from nonscientific ones is how vulnerable the ideas are allowed to be. Let's look at one more example.




Reasoning is the best guide we have to the truth... Those who offer alter -natives to reason are either mere hucksters, mere claimants to the throne, or there's a case to be made for them; and of course, that is an appeal to reason.  Michael Scriven, Reasoning

Suppose as a student of philosophy and science I become disillusioned by the fact that the average evangelist on Sunday morning TV can make hundreds of thousands of dollars, have a private jet airplane, lucrative tax write-offs, and live a far more comfortable life than the average college professor. So I decide to use my talents to create my own religion. Suppose a little market research reveals a sizable untapped portion of the population disillusioned with traditional religion. These people consider themselves very modern, have a passing interest in science and science fiction, but do not have a belief system that addresses their ultimate concerns and the meaning of life. What they need is a philosophical perspective or religion that places the past, present, and future in a meaningful whole: something that combines the security and comfort of traditional religion with modern technology and scientific understanding, making clear the purpose behind historical developments and our current potential.

Like Maupin I announce that I have had a vision, a vision based upon years of reading the texts of all the world's major religions. From the Bible, the Koran, and major Buddhist and Hindu texts I find passages that make plausible, with a little creative interpreting, that at this point in time the human species is ready, both spiritually and technologically, to fulfill life's ultimate mission. I show that all the world's religions have been hinting at the same conclusion. What was needed was the right time and a spiritual vehicle to read the texts correctly. I announce that God exists in the form of a Universal Consciousness or energy field, the same energy field that physicists only partly picture in their mathematical equations. Furthermore, I explain that many intelligent forms of life exist in the Universe and that a basic plan has been established for each one of them. Each must fulfill an innate potential before being reassimilated into this blissful Consciousness. Our beacon becomes the quote from the Bible, "In my Father's House there are many mansions." We have been given a spiritual and technological potential to realize. Actualizing this potential will enable us to eventually come face to face (mind to mind) with God.

As a demonstration of our development, we must literally find God. For us, God will appear in a form we can understand on a planet somewhere in the Universe in a resplendent pearl which is exactly ten times larger than any other pearl in the entire Universe. Our goal is to build the spacecraft based on present technology, "to boldly go where no human being has gone," and find this planet. All we need, of course, is about $240 from each of our "cosmo-partners" to start our final adventure.

Suppose my new religion is very successful. I raise large sums of money, the spaceships are built, and thousands of different groups of cosmo-partners begin to crisscross the galaxy. Finally, one day on a very remote planet a pearl exactly ten times larger than any other pearl is found. But, except for an electrical storm in Tucson Arizona, nothing happens. What would I say to my increasingly skeptical followers? I have many responses. First of all this is a sign. It is amazing that we found a pearl at all. We are on the right track. There must be another pearl ten times larger than this one. We must keep the faith, surely "some day" we will find the right pearl. Or, I could tell my followers that they blew it. They were face to face with God (it was the right pearl) but their lack of faith and skepticism caused God to move to another pearl and wait for a display of greater spiritual maturity on the part of my followers. I could turn their skepticism into guilt.

The problem with these beliefs is that they are logically irrefutable; they have been made invulnerable to disproof. They could be true, but no matter what happens, they cannot be proved false. A way out always exits -- an explanation to show that an inconsistency is only an apparent inconsistency. If a fact appears to be inconsistent with an irrefutable belief, we can always show it is only an apparent inconsistency and even turn it into a confirmation, a positive instance in favor of the belief. We just did not find the right pearl. But we are on the right track. After all we did find a pearl ten times larger than any other pearl. This proves there are pearls ten times larger than other pearls. So, we need to keep looking for another pearl ten times larger than the one we found. And so on. If a predicted date for a trip to heaven does not materialize, obviously there was human error in calculation. After all, there was an electrical storm. Is this not a sign that we are on the right track? If we build a Tower of Faith Research Center to cure cancer and people continue to get cancer, well obviously the collective faith of the human race is still weak and cannot completely fend off the work of the devil. We need more contributions from prayer partners to take our message to the world. And so on.

Psychologically, irrefutable beliefs are very compelling. Everything is explained: There is finality to our views, a secure feeling that allows us to face life's contingencies and uncertainties. Whatever happens can always be viewed as part of the plan. But when one irrefutable belief contradicts another, there is then no decisive way for a choice between them. With an irrefutable belief all that we are left with is a "maybe-belief" that may make us feel good.

By contrast, a scientific theory must be refutable in principle; a circumstance or a set of circumstances must potentially exist such that if observed would logically prove the theory wrong. To enter the arena of science a belief must be testable and there must be a limit to the explanatory "patches" or excuses we give when our theories fail tests. When two scientific theories offer different explanations, there must be a way of testing them and finding which one is better. Although there is much more to scientific discovery and justification than simply having beliefs and testing them, some philosophers of science refer to a logic in discussing scientific reasoning. This logic is often given an impressive name, known as the hypothetical-deductive process.

 Methodologies of Science

A simplified version of the logic of science can be summarized as follows. Scientists begin the encounter with nature by making careful observations. Somehow through a kind of creativity mill, a hypothesis is generated about how some process of nature works. On the basis of this hypothesis, a test or experiment is conducted by logically deducing a prediction -- a set of observations that should occur, under particular conditions, if the hypothesis is true. For instance, if the hypothesis states that all physically abused children grow up to be abusive parents, then it follows that a particular abused female child should be observed in the future to be an abusive mother. If the predicted event occurs, then the hypothesis is confirmed. If the prediction does not take place, then the hypothesis is disconfirmed.

This skeletal essence of science describes a simple logical process of confrontation between facts and hypotheses, but the actual process involves much creativity and auxiliary assumptions. How would child abuse be observed in a family situation without affecting the result? How would abuse be measured? If an abused child grows up and does not become an abusive parent, do we know our hypothesis is wrong or were we wrong about the child being truly abused? Was the hypothesis wrong or was the supposed fact wrong?

Furthermore, historically we know of situations in science where the hypothesis was right and the supposed fact was right, even though the fact appeared initially to disconfirm the hypothesis. When Newton's laws of motion were used to predict the movement and location of the planet Uranus, they did not work. Newton's laws had been so successful in so many situations that physicists were skeptical of believing the laws were wrong. So an auxiliary hypothesis was introduced to "save" Newton's laws, to reconcile the laws with the facts.  The French mathematician Le Verrier predicted in 1846 that an unseen planet existed beyond the orbit of Uranus that was gravitationally affecting Uranus's motion.  The planet Neptune was soon discovered, and what appeared to be a refutation of a hypothesis turned into a great vindication of the hypothesis by confirmation of the auxiliary hypothesis.

Thus, most philosophers of science today speak of a scientific theory being a "web" of beliefs. There may be a major hypothesis, but that hypothesis is connected to many auxiliary hypotheses and assumptions. If the overall set of beliefs makes a false prediction, all we know is that something is wrong with the whole package of beliefs. At least one of the beliefs or assumptions in the web is wrong.

The ancient Greek astronomer Aristarchus introduced the hypothesis of a moving Earth spinning on its axis and revolving around the Sun almost two thousand years before Copernicus did in the sixteenth century. But this hypothesis was rejected for what appeared at the time to be good scientific reasons. If the Earth was spinning at a thousand miles per hour, all physical objects on Earth should be drastically affected. We would fly off the Earth like children on a merrygoround spinning too fast. It was not until the seventeenth century that Newton's theory of gravity explained why we do not fly away.

Critics of science will claim there is no difference between a scientific theory and a religious claim such as Bill Maupin's. Maupin patched his web of beliefs with an auxiliary: "I got the date wrong." Supporters of Newton patched their theory with an auxiliary: "There must be another planet affecting Uranus's motion." This charge is serious and we will need to address the question whether scientific theories can also be made invulnerable to disproof through this patching process. We will do so in Chapter 5.

For now let us simply note that if the planet Neptune had not been found at the predicted location, physicists would not have been comfortable with the open-ended belief: "We know there must be a planet out there somewhere and some day we will find it." At some point it is more rational to reject a theory than continue to try to patch it. Otherwise, in the words of Pierre Duhem, our sets of beliefs begin to resemble "the worm-eaten columns of a building tottering in every part."(1)

In other words, without a disconfirmation being possible in principle, a belief is not acceptable as even a potential scientific hypothesis. Testability is a major standard in scientific practice. If a scientist attempts to patch a pet theory too much, if the patches are so open-ended that it appears that there is no hope of testing the patches, then the work of this scientist will not be taken seriously by the rest of the scientific community. Bottom line: The vulnerability of scientific beliefs forces us to be critical, to observe and test our ideas against the world.

Let's explore the critical attitude behind this process further with a few more examples. Consider the following letter to the editor in a daily newspaper:

2-1
Just why is everyone pushing this sex education in schools? Why is it necessary? The worn-out reason is that a lot of parents do not talk about it at home, therefore it must be taught in school. Yet, since this trend started, VD and pregnancy among teenagers and even preteens has sharply gone up. Why then? I thought sex education was supposed to reduce it, not increase it. The answer is that it is a "how-to-do-it" course, nothing else. That is why the dirty books are being pushed in schools now to go with it. Sex education is bunk.  

Suppose the observation is accurate. There has been a significant rise in venereal disease and pregnancies among teenagers simultaneous with the introduction of sex education courses in public schools. Even so, what for this person is proof that there is a causal connection between sex education in schools and a recent rise in venereal disease and teenage pregnancy is only an observation of a possible correlation from a scientific point of view.

It is important to note that what for most people is the conclusion of an investigation is only the beginning of an investigation for science. Many other factors could cause the rise of teenage VD and pregnancies. A rise in the population of teenagers is possible, causing everything that teenagers do to go up: automobile accidents, the purchasing of particular types of clothing, music CDs and tapes, and acne lotion for example. Few would claim that teaching sex education in schools has been the cause of increased purchases of acne lotion. There also could be an increase in the population of particular types of teenagers, those in an area of the country where sex education is not taught or where early sexual experimentation is encouraged by various social or family pressures. Correlation does not prove causation. A correlation between sex education and teen sex problems does not prove a causal connection, and by itself, it does not give us a clear indication in which direction there may be a connection. Knowing only the basic correlation, it is possible that an increase in teen sex problems caused an increase in sexual education classes!

Unlike the religious visions discussed earlier, this claim is at least testable. We can imagine a set of possible observable circumstances that would prove this belief false.

Suppose a group of scientists apply for a government grant to study the possibility that public sex education classes have produced great harm. Suppose the time is right. The country is in a conservative mood, and many people believe that the liberalism of the 1960s has produced many government intrusions into what are purely personal or family matters.

The political and cultural environment are very important as a backdrop to scientific investigations. Perhaps the social science community has been too liberal to recognize the possible connection between sex education and teenage sex problems. Similarly, personal bias against sex education probably allowed our letter writer to recognize the correlation in the first place. Ideas do not emerge from a vacuum or a purely unbiased state of mind, or purely from objective observations as portrayed in our brief description of the hypothetical-deductive process. Ideas must often be popular or controversial before they are studied.

Critics of science will claim that history shows that often beliefs were accepted by scientists as true before crucial evidential support for them was found. Galileo and Kepler, for example, believed in a moving Earth decades before the evidence was conclusive from a modern perspective. These same critics will claim that scientific objectivity is a myth and that evidence is actually socially constructed by what emerges as the most popular view of the day from struggles for power within a society. In Chapter 5 we will see that insofar as science is an activity carried out by human beings, such human factors as cultural and political influence can never be eliminated. We will also claim, however, that such human factors are often unexpectedly helpful to scientific discovery, that they give the logic of science life and direction. Initial biases are not necessarily bad as is often supposed. Biases can help us see things that otherwise may have been missed. We will also see that science adopts particular values. Science is not value-neutral as is often proposed, but we will see that having such values is essential to the objective operation of scientific methods. For the moment though, let's return to our study.

If the scientists received a grant, they would conduct a controlled study. As noted, there could be many other causes of the increase in teen pregnancies and VD. The possible causes are called variables. The goal of a controlled study is to control as many of these variables as possible, so that given two populations of teenagers all the possible variables are the same overall except one -- only one population will have had sex education in school. In this way, if there is a significant difference in the percentages of pregnancies and VD in the group that had sex education, we will then have a more reasonable basis for claiming that there is a causal connection between sex education classes in public schools and subsequent teen sex problems. If we find no significant difference between the two groups, it would be reasonable to conclude that a population increase or some other causal factor is involved.

Even though our scientists may receive a large grant from the government, they cannot survey every teenager in the country. They must be content with a sample, a sample that matches in characteristics the total population of teenagers. Such a sample is called a representative sample, and standard techniques exist for producing one. Political pollsters are able to interview between 1,000 and 1,500 carefully chosen people and, on this basis, predict the overall voting habits of over 50 million people.

Suppose then that after a great deal of research two representative groups of 1,000 teenagers each are identified. Each group has characteristics evenly distributed. There are as many boys as girls; poor, middle class, and wealthy; rural, urban, and suburban. The only significant difference known between the two groups is that the teenagers in one group have had a sex education class and the teenagers of the other group have never had a sex education class. If there is a much larger percentage of problems in the sex education group, the original hypothesis will be confirmed. If there is no significant difference in the percentages, the hypothesis will be disconfirmed. What if there is a larger percentage of problems in the group without sex education? The government sponsors would probably be very unhappy, but many social scientists would likely view this result as evidence for the hypothesis that sex education classes are helping prevent teenage sex problems, and the increase must be due to other factors.

In this study our scientists would probably already have data on which teenagers had become pregnant, and which ones had not, or had an episode of VD, and which ones did not. The study would be retrospective. It is usually more practical to first identify the results or effects -- in this case, teenagers who have become pregnant or have VD -- and then trace out the histories behind these effects. This method weakens the conclusion somewhat. The past must be reconstructed from data that is available in the present. Questionnaires and records must be used that may be biased or inaccurate.

In the case of a questionnaire, the results can easily be biased by the questions asked. For instance, Ann Landers once conducted a survey in her syndicated column. She asked women, "Would you be content to be held close and treated tenderly, and forget about the act?" Not surprisingly, out of 90,000 women that responded, over 60,000 chose "tenderness" and "closeness." Her question implied that sexual intercourse cannot be a vehicle for communication, love, care and emotional expression between couples, and is only an animalistic act needed and desired by the male of our species. Similarly, in our example a slanted question could cause a teenager to distort whether he or she had a sex education class. If the question is asked in such a way as to indicate approval for having had a sex education class, then a student who may already be feeling guilty about his or her situation is less likely to be truthful, not wanting to admit an ignorance of the facts of life. On the other hand, if the question reflects disapproval, a student is more likely to indicate that he or she has not had a sex education class. What would the results be like if the question asked was, "When you were younger, were you forced by a teacher to learn about the act?"

A better method, but one that is usually more expensive and time consuming, is a prospective study. Rather than recording present incidents of teenage sex problems and then infer from a backwards-in-time analysis to the possible cause, a prospective study attempts to gain more control over the present and then allows nature to take its course, the results being observed in the future. In our example, this would mean finding a thousand students who will soon have a sex education class, and a thousand who will not. Again, ideally, there would be no other significant difference between the two groups. We would then wait five years or so, and analyze the percentage of sex problems of the two groups. Such a study would not only be logically tighter, because of greater control and observational closeness to the original groups, but the results would then be used to corroborate the retrospective study if they were the same. An independent study would exist with the same conclusion.

Scientists never consider one study conclusive. The results of a single study are only suggestive. A single study is analyzed carefully by other members of the scientific community, critiqued, and on the basis of this critique, other independent studies are conducted. The new studies will often have tighter controls, addressing weaknesses overlooked in the initial study. It is very important that scientific studies be replicated. They must be repeated many times by different investigators using different approaches before the scientific community arrives at a consensus. As noted earlier, what for many people is the conclusion of an investigation is but the beginning of a patient, methodical investigation for the scientific community.

For this reason you should be wary of dramatic newspaper headlines that report the results of a single scientific result. Establishing reliable connections in nature takes time. Although most scientists believe that the world is governed by simple truths, on the surface it is a complicated place, and remember that any experiment or test of a hypothesis is actually a test of a complex web or set of hypotheses. In our example, the focus was on whether sex education causes teen sex problems, but much more than this is being tested. We are also testing the result of when students have sex education, and how they have it. Having a sex education class in the 5th grade may be very different than in the 6th or 7th grade. The type of sex education class is also be important. Was it taught by a woman or a man? Did the teacher make use of visual aids and films? Did the program focus on crucial psycho-social variables, such as assertiveness training for girls and goal-setting for girls and boys?  Obviously the logic of a scientific study itself is much more complicated than simply stating that if our hypothesis is true, then such and such circumstances should be observed under controlled conditions. If the results do not occur as predicted, all that can be concluded logically is that something is wrong somewhere in the total set of hypotheses and assumptions we used to deduce what should happen.

Furthermore, facts are seldom just facts. Observations must be interpreted. For instance, suppose within two years of having a sex education class a girl becomes pregnant. Unanalyzed her pregnancy is just a statistic, apparently against the hypothesis that such classes should help prevent ten pregnancy. But suppose the pregnancy was the result of rape?

Initial scientific results are seen as suggestive but inconclusive. Scientists have learned that nature does not reveal its secrets easily. They have learned to be shy suitors in their romance with the Universe. Through countless experiences of having their ideas rejected by nature, they have learned to be very cautious in proclaiming what we know. The game of science requires great patience. Like many fishermen on a shoreline, we throw out our net of ideas on the limitless sea of nature hoping to catch a few of nature's secrets. Most of the time we pull in empty nets, and it requires a whole community of fishermen, cooperating and communicating, constantly critiquing and evaluating each other's fishing technique, to accumulate anything substantial.

One of the best examples of years of scientific patience and overwhelming corroboration is the discovered connection between cigarette smoking and lung cancer. In the 1970s the warning label on a package of cigarettes was changed from "Caution, smoking may be hazardous to your health" to "Caution, smoking is hazardous to your health." This change showed that within the scientific community a consensus had been reached that, among other things, cigarette smoking is the principal factor in causing lung cancer.

What exactly does this mean? It does not mean that every person who smokes cigarettes will get lung cancer. To say, as is believed today, that 80% of all lung cancer cases are caused by smoking, is not the same as saying that 80% of the people who smoke will have, and eventually die of, lung cancer. Some smokers may die of other causes, such as other forms of cancer, some may suffer accidental deaths, and some may die of old age without having any form of cancer. However, it is believed that cigarette smokers are about 140 times more likely than nonsmokers to get lung cancer. This claim implies that it is possible to have lung cancer and not smoke, and not have lung cancer and smoke. The possibility of dying from lung cancer is very low if you do not smoke, and the possibility of dying from lung cancer is also somewhat low if you do smoke. But the possibility of dying of lung cancer if you do not smoke is so low, that even though the possibility of dying of lung cancer as a smoker is also low, it is 140 times higher.

Many decades ago public health officials began to notice a significant increase in the number of lung cancer cases at the same time cigarette smoking was increasing. In the 1930's there were about 3 lung cancer deaths per 100,000 persons per year. By 1955 this had increased to 25 deaths per 100,000 persons per year. At first this was primarily a male disease when many more men smoked than women did; as more and more women began to smoke the death rate for women began to increase. Of course, air pollution, the use of automobiles, daily stress, and the pace of life had also increased. Thus, as in our sex education example, this was only a good place to start an investigation, not conclude one. At this point, the evidence linking smoking and lung cancer was only circumstantial.

One of the first systematic scientific studies was directed by the medical doctors, E.C. Hammond and D. Horn.(2) Throughout the United States 180,000 men between the ages of 50 and 69 were interviewed, targeting their smoking habits, general health, and living environment. At the beginning of the study none of the men had any serious illness. Ten years later the death rate due to lung cancer between those men who smoked and those that did not was compared. To simplify the results, the statistics can be presented from the point of view of the proportion of deaths per 100,000 men. In the group of men who had never smoked, 34 cases of lung cancer resulted per 100,000 men over a ten year period. In the group of men who had smoked cigarettes, 4,719 cases of lung cancer resulted per 100,000 men over a ten year period. Furthermore, the study showed that the more a man smoked -- from less than one-half a pack per day to more than two packs per day -- the more likely he would be one of the 4,719 after a ten year period. This was a significant difference (140 times). Could the high percentage of lung cancer deaths among the men who smoked be a coincidence?

The Hammond and Horn study can be criticized on several points, something the tobacco companies were quick to do. Although a basic environmental variable was controlled -- as many men lived in the city and the country in both groups -- many other important factors were not controlled. For instance, it was possible that all of the men who died of lung cancer, including those who had never smoked, had a diet that was significantly different than those who did not die of the disease. Or they may have had unhealthy occupations, whether in the country or the city, that placed an unusual amount of stress on the lungs. Perhaps all the men with cancer had very stressful occupations involving a great deal of daily nervous tension. Finally, and this has been a point the tobacco companies have used consistently, the variable of heredity was not controlled. That is, it is possible that all the men who had cancer were prone to some form of cancer genetically, and this would be revealed by finding other incidents of cancer in their family histories. Parents, grandparents, and relatives of the men who succumbed to lung cancer might have shown a large percentage of cancer, if this was investigated.

Such criticisms serve a very positive function. Critiques that reveal weaknesses serve as a basis to make the next study better. Since the Hammond and Horn study, thousands of similar studies have been conducted, each incorporating tighter and tighter controls based on possible oversights of the previous studies. By the early 1980s studies included controls on all of the variables mentioned above. Yet the results have been the same. By 1990 over 140,000 people a year died of lung cancer, an impressive majority smoked cigarettes, and for the first time lung cancer for women exceeded that of breast cancer.(3) Other diverse corroborating factors have also been identified, such as the chemical analysis of cigarette smoke revealing over 200 toxic substances, including radioactivity.  (For more on the dangers of smoking cigarettes, see the story of super model Christy Turlington.)

Does this prove that cigarette smoking is the principal cause of lung cancer? For logically technical reasons it does not. In fact, there is no such thing as a scientific proof, if proof means something that is known with absolute logical certainty. A controlled study can never be completely controlled. The number of ways people can differ, the number of possible variables, is infinite. In all the studies on cigarette smoking, it is possible that some obscure third factor was the real culprit. All of the people who had lung cancer could possibly have had some subtle factor in common that went unrecognized in every study. Perhaps the real cause was that all of the people who had lung cancer were given a piece of bubble gum on their fifth birthday, and it just so happened there was a chemical in the gum that reacted with their bodies in such a way that it made them susceptible to a slow virus, which then led to lung cancer fifty or so years later! This and millions of other "off the wall" possibilities have never been tested. The link between smoking and lung cancer cannot be known in the sense of "known beyond any logical or conceivable doubt." The point is, however, can we say we know that cigarette smoking is a principal cause of lung cancer beyond a "reasonable doubt"? Is it rational to claim to know something even if we are not absolutely sure that we know something? Can we distinguish between what is "conceivably" true and what is "reasonably" true?


A Little Logic






The goal of inductive reasoning must be to make the conclusion as strong as possible; to show that a belief is a reliable belief and unlikely to be false given the evidence.






















We have to live today by what truth we can get today, and be ready tomorrow to call it falsehood.  William James
























































No belief supported by science can be said ever to be certain.  Certainty is not the issue. The issue is whether some beliefs are more reliable and practical than others.





























Even though it is possible that cigarette smoking does not cause lung cancer, given the available evidence rational human beings know that they have a reliable basis to believe that they should not smoke if they desire to live a long healthy life. Similarly, given the evidence found by astronomers from all over the world, it is very unlikely that the Universe is only a few thousand years old as some religious groups still maintain.




































Scientists believe many strange things, but not without reason. For the vulnerability of testable ideas forces upon scientists a cooperative, critical process, whereby they must confront, observe, and be more intimate with the world, rather than veil it with ideas that seem philosophically satisfying or comforting.











Urging people not to believe Darwin's theory is true is as dangerous as urging people not to believe in universal gravitation and that they can hover outside the windows of tall buildings.








Science is based on particular cognitive value judgments. Scientists have learned to value curiosity, criticism, testability, open mindedness and accepting beliefs as tentative. They have learned to value a life-style and a process of interacting with the world that are on-going.
 In the philosophy of science this knowledge challenge is known as the problem of induction. Using inductive reasoning no matter how much evidence we have for a conclusion, the conclusion could still conceivably be false. Unlike deductive reasoning, which preserves certainty, if all our evidence is true, we can at best say that it is "unlikely" that our conclusion is false when using inductive reasoning. To understand this, first consider the following deductive argument.

2-2
All U.S.-manufactured cars built after 2004 were equipped with tire pressure warning lights at the factory.
John has a 2005 U.S.-manufactured Ford.
Thus, John's car was equipped with tire pressure warning lights at the factory.

If the first two statements are true (logicians call these statements premises), then the conclusion must be true. We may not know for sure if the premises are true, or we may find out that the conclusion is false, but there is no doubt that the conclusion is true, if the premises are true. John could possibly have a 2003 Ford, or the year when all cars were equipped with tire pressure warning lights might not be 2004.  But if it were true that every U.S. manufactured car from 2004 on was indeed equipped with tire pressure warning lights at the factory, and John has a car built after 2004, then there would be no doubt that he has one of the cars that was equipped with tire pressure warning lights at the factory. If we discover that he does not have a car that was equipped with tire pressure warning lights at the factory, then we can conclude that at least one of the premises is false. Deductive reasoning provides certainty. Either the conclusion is known to be true, if the premises are true, or, at least one of the premises is known to be false, if the conclusion is known to be false. With valid deductive reasoning, it is not possible to have true premises and a false conclusion.

Contrast this with the following example of inductive reasoning.

2-3
We have hiked in this forest all day, observing many locations, and we have not seen a single hummingbird. Therefore, probably no hummingbirds live in this forest.

Notice that the conclusion is a considerable generalization. Inductive conclusions always go beyond the evidence provided in the premises. Inductive arguments can be good or bad, strong or weak, but even if they are strong, the conclusion could still be false. Thus, the goal of inductive reasoning must be to make the conclusion as strong as possible; to show that a belief is a reliable belief and unlikely to be false given the evidence. In the above example, the conclusion would be better supported if the observers were specialists in hummingbird habitation, and if the forest were observed for many days. Even so, the conclusion could still be false.

Life is full of uncertainty and all our most important beliefs are fallible. However, reasonable people draw reasonable inductive conclusions from only some evidence every day. We reason from the past to the present, and the past and the present to the future.

2-4
Lana studied hard for her last test and received a high grade. So, if she studies hard for her next test, she has a good chance of a high grade.

We reason from some examples of a population to all or most examples of a population.

2-5
In a sample of 1200 representative voters nationwide 40% said that they will vote for Jack Kemp for president. So, it is likely that Jack Kemp will get approximately 40% of the vote for president if the election were held today.

And we reason from a sequence of events to a causal connection between those events.

2-6
We felt an earthquake and shortly after the building across the street collapsed. Thus, it is likely that the earthquake caused the building to collapse.

To understand more thoroughly the difference between weak inductive inferences and strong ones consider the following example.

Suppose we bring into a room a barrel full of apples. Suppose someone tells us that there are 100 apples in the barrel. I reach into the barrel, pull out one apple from the top, and place it on a desk. Suppose we can see that no one in his right mind would eat this apple. It is considerably overripe and is soft and full of maggots. Although few people would purchase the barrel of apples for human consumption, few also would be willing to wager their life savings from this single apple that we know all the apples in the barrel are rotten. On the basis of only a single positive instance, would it be reasonable to accept the generalization that all the apples are rotten? On the basis of this single piece of evidence would it be wise to conclude that this is a reliable belief?

Small amounts of evidence need not always be weak. A microbiologist, for instance, might be willing on the basis of this one apple to wager that all of the apples are likely to be rotten, if other information were provided. Given the general knowledge of the existence of bacteria and their ability to spread rapidly, if it were known at what temperature the apples were stored, and for how long, a conclusion that all the apples are rotten might not be totally unreasonable. Often in science a very small number of observations are made to do a lot of hypothetical work, especially when they are placed within a framework of a general consensus of belief of how the world works. This general consensus is sometimes called background knowledge by philosophers of science, and background beliefs are part of the web of beliefs used in testing hypotheses. If these beliefs are also well tested and highly corroborated, the evidential weight of a few facts can increase substantially. For instance, suppose it was well known by apple farmers that barrels of apples always rot from the bottom up. The single case of a rotten apple from the top now has more weight. In the example above about earthquakes, the conclusion is given more weight because of our general knowledge from past experience that earthquakes can cause lots of damage to human infrastructure.

Without anything else to go on though, concluding that all the apples are rotten from a single positive case is a very weak inductive inference. To make the inductive inference stronger more apples need to be sampled. If I pulled out four more, for a total of five, and all of them were just like the first one, we would now have a better basis for concluding that all the apples are rotten. The process of adding positive cases for a generalization is called induction by enumeration. In general, the more positive cases in favor of a hypothesis, the stronger the hypothesis.

Yet if all five were simply pulled off the top, it is still possible that the apples on the bottom are not rotten. Thus, a stronger case could be made by choosing a representative sample, by selecting one from the top, one from the very bottom, one from each side of the barrel, and one from the middle of the barrel. If all five are rotten, this would strengthen the hypothesis considerably. As noted previously, political pollsters know that we are much more likely to know the likelihood of the voting behavior of 50 million voters by examining a representative sample of 1200 voters distributed throughout the country, rather than 100,000 people living in New York. A small representative sample is much is better than a large unrepresentative one. Five representative apples are better than 20 just off the top.

Suppose we continued to sample the apples by pulling out 40 more. It would be much more likely that all the apples are rotten, if all 50 sampled were rotten. But would you bet your life savings on the proposition that all the apples remaining are rotten? Probably not, because we know that it is still possible that some, even many, of the remaining apples are not rotten. Suppose we pulled out 49 more. Suppose that all 99 sampled are rotten. Do we know with certainty that the last remaining apple is rotten? It is still possible the hypothesis, "all the apples are rotten," is false, even though we have an overwhelming number of positive cases supporting it.

Much about the logic of science can be summarized with reference to this little example. Earlier we mentioned that science uses a hypothetical-deductive process. It is given this name because starting with a hypothesis, a prediction is deduced about what should be observed under particular conditions if the hypothesis is true. If the hypothesis is that all the apples in the barrel are rotten, then one selected from the middle should be rotten. The entire process is based on induction, however, because if an apple is pulled from the middle of the barrel and observed to be rotten, this confirms the hypothesis, but it does not logically prove it. Understand that it is possible to deduce true conclusions (an apple will be rotten in the middle of the barrel) from premises that may be false (all the apples are rotten).

Viewed from the perspective of deductive reasoning, the situation would appear even more discouraging. Scientific reasoning would appear to commit the logical fallacy of affirming the consequent. Consider these examples.

2-7
Anyone who lives in California also lives in the United States. Jorge lives in the United States. So, Jorge lives in California.

2-8
If all the apples in the barrel are rotten, then all the apples sampled will be rotten. All the apples sampled are rotten. So, all the apples in the barrel are rotten.

Viewed only in this limited way, scientific reasoning would appear to be irrational. From a deductive point of view, both of the above are examples of poor reasoning. The premises could be true, but the conclusions false. Critics of science will attempt to make much of this logical situation. Rationally, they will claim, we are free to believe anything, and any belief can be made to work. Consider these famous examples.

2-9
Every emerald examined in the world thus far has been green. Therefore, it is likely that all emeralds are green.

2-10
Every emerald examined in the world thus far has been green. Therefore, it is likely that all emeralds are grue. (Grue is defined as an emerald that is green before the year 2100, but turns blue after the year 2100.)

Critics of science will maintain that these examples show that any fact used to support a particular hypothesis can also be used to support a different hypothesis. An observational equivalence exists for the above hypotheses. All the observations made in support of the green hypothesis also support the grue hypothesis equally.

Supporters of science respond that these criticisms apply the wrong standard to scientific reasoning. No belief supported by science can be said ever to be certain. Certainty is not the issue. The perspective should be more pragmatic. In the real world, removed from ivory tower logical debates, the issue is whether some beliefs are better supported than others by the available evidence and thus should be used as a basis for decision and action. The issue is whether some beliefs can attain the status of a reliable belief, a belief that although not certain, nevertheless is so well supported by evidence that it serves as a practical basis for action. Consider some more examples.

2-11
"Hana is probably having an affair with Roberto," concluded Hana's boyfriend Sam. "When I called Hana this evening, her phone was busy. Then when I called Roberto his phone was also busy."

2-12
Hansen most likely killed Kaneshiro. His finger prints were on the gun that killed Kaneshiro. Hansen was seen buying the gun the morning of the murder at a gun shop on 5th street. Hansen had vowed publicly that he would kill Kaneshiro some day for stealing his business. Furthermore, Hansen was seen by four independent witnesses leaving Kaneshiro's office shortly after gun shots were heard.

2-13
If gravity is a universal force that applies everywhere on Earth, then all heavy objects dropped thus far from tall buildings will fall down. All heavy objects dropped thus far from tall buildings have fallen down. Thus, gravity is a universal force that applies everywhere on Earth.

The inductive inference in example 2-12 is surely much stronger than that in 2-11, and would serve as a better basis for action. If you were on a jury, you would have a good set of reasons to vote for Hansen's guilt; whereas if Sam acted on his inference in the first example it would most likely be disastrous. Example 2-12 is the fallacy of affirming the consequent, but clearly shows that, in the words of Pierre Duhem, "Pure logic is not the only rule for our judgments"(4); that we must view inductive inferences from a much wider context than deductive logic.

The belief that gravity will make every heavy object fall from tall buildings is a huge generalization that goes beyond the available evidence. Think of all the locations outside windows in tall buildings in which objects have never been dropped. Because there are many places that have never been tested, it is possible that one of these locations is mystically special and a heavy object will not fall when dropped. But what person in his or her right mind would be willing to test this possibility with his or her body? I know I will fall to my death if I jump out a window on the 50th floor of a building. I have knowledge even though my belief is not certain. It would be very wise for me to act on this belief and not do something stupid, even though I do not have absolute certainty that the belief is true. I have a reliable, practical belief, even though it is fallible.

True it is possible to deduce true predictions from a false theory, so even our best supported inductive inferences must be viewed as tentative. No matter how long a theory has been successful in making predictions, it cannot be known to be true absolutely. It could be found to be false tomorrow. In our relationship with nature the logical situation is much worse than our apple example. In nature the situation is analogous to a barrel full of an infinite number of apples. In the cigarette smoking case there are over a billion people who smoke and they differ in countless ways. Every scientific conclusion mentioned in this book could be wrong. It is possible that cigarette smoking is not a principal cause of lung cancer. It is even possible that having lung cancer causes some people to smoke! It is possible that the Universe is not 14 billion years old and as large as scientists think it is. The issue, however, is not possibility but reliability. Even though it is possible that cigarette smoking does not cause lung cancer, given the available evidence rational human beings know that they have a reliable basis to believe that they should not smoke if they desire to live a long healthy life. Similarly, given the evidence found by astronomers from all over the world, it is very unlikely that the Universe is only a few thousand years old as some religious groups still maintain.

Critics of science will often attempt to use this logical weakness in inductive reasoning to repudiate many scientific conclusions. In the next chapter we will see that one of the main arguments offered by creationists against the theory of evolution amounts to no more than saying the theory is based on induction. They argue that evolution is "only a theory," it cannot be known to be true, so it must be based on a leap of faith.(5) Wrong. Such reasoning reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of the goal of scientific methods. Darwin's theory of natural selection is one of the most factually supported and independently corroborated theories of all science. The available evidence is overwhelming that it is a reliable belief. In the next chapter we will show that urging people not to believe it is as dangerous as urging people not to believe in universal gravitation and that they can hover outside the windows of tall buildings. Thinking this way is bound to lead to disastrous personal and social decisions.

Critics of science fail also to recognize the positive aspect of the fallibility of scientific methods. Without room for doubt, there would be no room for self-correction, and we would be left with a cluttered clash of irrefutable beliefs. The process of criticism and doubt may not always be comfortable, but as noted by Xenophanes in the opening quote to this chapter, "in the course of time, through seeking, men find that which is better." Thus we see that science is based on particular cognitive value judgments. Scientists have learned to value curiosity, criticism, testability, openmindedness and accepting beliefs as tentative. They have learned to value a life-style and a process of interacting with the world that are on-going.(6) They believe these values are positive, because in the long run they produce growth, even though the process is one of insecurity and fallibility.

We cannot analyze the evidence for every scientific claim cited in this book, but keep in mind, as we survey the worldview of modern science and its historical development in the following chapters, that this patient critical attitude lies behind all the scientific explanations discussed, no matter how strange. Science does not claim to know all the answers. It does, however, claim to provide us with methods of test and reliable interaction by which we can become more and more intimate with the Universe.

Because scientists are human beings, many aspects of our humanity also play a role in scientific discovery: artistic creation and imagination, political manipulation and personal exploitation, wishful thinking, bias, egocentricity, hypercritical review and premature skeptical rejection. There are no absolute truths.(7) Every solution to a mystery creates new mysteries; the Universe is an eternal riddle for which there are no final solutions. Science is a game that never ends, a game whose completion would render, as the ancient Greek philosophers believed, life boring.


Epistemology

There is nothing so absurd or incredible that it has not been asserted by one philosopher or another.  Rene Descartes









What is necessary "for the very existence of science," and what the characteris -tics of nature are, are not determined by pompous preconditions, they are determined always by the material with which we work, by nature herself.  Richard Feynman, The Character of Physical Law









Science is based on empiricism.  Beliefs must be validated by experiences that are public.  They must be objective. . . Galileo's observations were objective and public. Anyone who kept an open mind should be able to look through a telescope and see the same thing.

In the last section we began to discuss what philosophers call epistemology. Epistemology is a field in philosophy concerned with the study of knowledge itself, particularly the best way of knowing. I have defended an epistemological theory: The concept of a reliable belief -- the notion that some beliefs are so well supported by the available evidence that they serve as a practical basis for action, even though we cannot prove absolutely that they are true. This position can be seen as a compromise position between two extremes: Relativism which states that all beliefs are equally true, and Absolutism, which states that some beliefs can be known to be true with absolute certainty. I have also defended a process for arriving at reliable beliefs. I have made a normative claim: This process is better than other ways used for making knowledge claims.

There are different ways people claim to know things. Some of our beliefs are based upon testimony. Some one may tell me that New Orleans has excellent restaurants, or that Los Angeles has many freeways. If I believe these claims, but have never been to these cities, my belief is based on testimony. Sometimes the testimony is based upon authority, as would be the case if a chef told me about a good restaurant in New Orleans. Many times people accept beliefs based on popularity, as when a restaurant is believed to be a good restaurant simply because many people say it is a good restaurant. Many religions claim that a valid method of knowing involves revelation, whereby important truths about life, impossible to find out any other way, are disclosed to human beings by a divine being or God. Bill Maupin claimed to have had a mystical vision. Mystics, in general, claim that after years of special training it is possible to know some very important things about life and the Universe "intuitively" while in a deep state of meditation. These intuitive visions should not be confused with revelation, because they not only involve personal effort and training, but also do not necessarily involve divine aid or a God. Mystics claim that these special perceptions are repeatable. That is, any human being can have these experiences if they follow the proper training long enough. Oral Roberts, however, claimed to have a personal experience that was not repeatable. Only he, so he claimed, talked directly to God.

Scientists do not believe it is possible to know if life exists on Europa by intuition, revelation, testimony, authority, or popularity. They believe that objective observational experience is necessary, either indirectly via a robot machine with sensors and cameras or directly by putting human observers there. Science is based on the epistemological position known as empiricism. Beliefs must be validated by experiences that are public. They must be objective.

Galileo Galilei is often cited in introductory science texts as being the first complete, empirical scientist. Instead of accepting ideas as true based upon authority or revelation, in 1609 he used a telescope to observe the Moon's surface. He saw that it was quite similar to the mountainous terrain of the Earth. He also observed that the planet Jupiter had at least four moons, that Venus had phases like that of the Moon; he even witnessed dark spots on the Sun. Such observations seemed to contradict both the authority of the ancient Aristotelian view of the Universe, which taught that heavenly bodies like the Moon and planets were celestial, not physical, places, and what was thought to be the revealed word of Biblical scripture, that the Earth was the center of God's Universe. Aristotelian doctrine also taught that lighter bodies would tend to go upward and heavier ones downward. So, it was assumed that a heavy body would fall faster than a light one. Instead of merely accepting the authority of Aristotle, instead of accepting this popular and commonplace belief, Galileo showed that actual measurements of falling bodies did not support Aristotle's belief. Thus, modern scientists believe that Galileo's work represents an epistemological revolution, where from this point on objective observational experience became the key method for attaining truth.

Note that Galileo's observations were objective and public. Anyone who kept an open mind should be able to look through a telescope and see the same thing. Unlike the experience of Oral Roberts, Galileo's observations are repeatable by any other human being. Public observations are also fallible. We could all be wrong about what we see through a telescope and any observation involves interpretation. However, modern empiricists believe that given the alternatives public observational experiences are the most reliable basis upon which to support beliefs.

Mystics will claim that they too are empiricists. They claim that only through personal experience can the important truths of life be known and that anyone with the proper training can experience these truths. Furthermore, mystics claim that this training, although different in kind, is no different in degree than that necessary for a scientist to interpret complicated data from a robot sensor. Many scientists scoff at this, because mystics will admit that these truths can only be experienced personally and cannot be communicated or described in a public language. Some scientists, a very distinct minority, say they are openminded about the possibility of such personal knowledge.8 But all scientists are united in belief that even if there is such knowledge, it is not scientific knowledge. To make a genuine scientific claim that claim must be testable by public observational experience.

 













That a belief is inconsistent with our common observa -tional experience is not by itself a conclusive argument that it is false.
































In trying to distinguish appearance from reality and lay bare the fundamental structure of the universe, science has had to transcend the "rabble of the senses."  Lincoln Barnet

An epistemological position opposed to empiricism, but historically very much influenced by the discovery and development of mathematics, is called rationalism. The rationalist believes that genuine knowledge is not possible without certainty and certainty is only achievable through the use of logic and reason. Rationalists argue that the result of public observational experiences can be wrong. We could all be wrong about what we see through a telescope, but we cannot be wrong that 2 + 2 = 4. Thus, the rationalist believes that the most important things about life, or at least the starting place for knowing the basic premises, can be known only through an intuition of self-evident certainty. Some statements are so clear logically or mathematically that we just know they are true. For instance, don't we know that there are no round squares on the dark side of the Moon? We don't need robot sensors and observational experience to know with certainty that a round square is impossible. Don't we know that it is impossible for a women to be born after her son has died? In short, the rationalist believes that we can know some things about life ahead of time, so to speak; we can know some things that no conceivable experience will contradict.

The ancient Greek philosophers Zeno and Parmenides (fifth-century B.C.) believed that our senses cannot be trusted. Sticks in water appear bent. Pulled out of the water, they are straight again. It is the rational ability of the human mind that enables us to know that the stick is not really bent. If we were limited to only observational experience, we would have to conclude that the stick bends when put into water and magically straightens itself out when pulled out.

Similarly, Zeno and Parmenides argued that our fundamental everyday experience of motion is an illusion. Making use of an apparent inconsistency between what our experience indicates is true and what logic dictates must be true, Zeno gave the following argument, known to this day as Zeno's paradox. To move from any point A to another point B, a person must first travel half the distance. To move from this half-way point to point B, again half the distance must be traversed, and so on. To move from any point to point B, again half the distance must be traversed. No matter how close point B is approached, there will still be some distance left, and the next half distance must be travelled again, and again, and again. Point B can never be reached. Since point B can be any distance, including a very short distance, it is thus logically impossible to move to even the shortest distance! We may experience ourselves and others moving about from point to point every day, but careful logical inspection, according to Zeno, reveals such movement to be an illusion. According to Zeno and Parmenides, since our experience is often mistaken, we should follow the logical guide of our reason, no matter how strange the result.

This paradox may seem silly to people in the twenty-first century. We move quite successfully every day between our homes, schools, and stores, and space shuttle flights that travel thousands of miles are routine. We should keep in mind, however, that it was very difficult for people living during the Middle Ages, and probably still is for many people today, to imagine that the Earth is moving and not the Sun. We do not experience ourselves moving at 1,000 miles per hour, instead we see the Sun move. That a belief is inconsistent with our common observational experience is not by itself a conclusive argument that it is false. The desk that I am writing this on appears to me to be very solid. Yet the atomic physicist informs us that the desk is 99.9% empty space. The "solidity" is another illusion due to the electromagnetic field generated by the electrons in the surface atoms of the desk and my hand. Because like electromagnetic forces repel each other, I cannot pass my hand through the table. It would be a very strange world without electromagnetic forces.

An empirical scientist would claim that we have used experience to learn these things. If this is true, it is one of the great ironies of science that it uses experience to prove other common place experiences to be illusions.

Empiricists also believe that some statements can be known with certainty. The difference is that the empiricist does not believe anything significant about the real world is conveyed by such statements. The only statements about the world that can be known ahead of time, prior to experiencing the world, are statements like "it is raining or not raining outside." Such statements are known to be true prior to checking the outside world only because they cover all possible results. Empirical scientists also believe that motion is real. But because of their faith in the ability of the human mind to figure things out, to use reason to peel away layers of illusion and ignorance, Zeno's ideas were taken very seriously by the early Greeks, even if they did not all agree with Zeno's conclusion that this proved motion and the physical world to be an illusion. For the early Greeks, such a fundamental inconsistency between common sense and reason, or any paradox for that matter, was nature's way of taunting us, of revealing an important one of her secrets. As we will see in Chapter 4, when we discuss the contribution of the philosopher Plato to our scientific culture, the early Greeks had tremendous confidence in the logical and mathematical powers of human thinking. Such confidence was a key ingredient in the development of modern science.

The intellectual forces necessary to produce our modern scientific revolution were not in place until the seventeenth century Renaissance period when a synthesis of rationalism and empiricism took place. The hypothetical-deductive process combines the logic of the rationalist (deduction) and the observational experience of the empiricist (induction). Before continuing with this epistemological discussion, let's look at a powerful example of this early faith in the power of reason and experience. In the process we will also see another indispensable part of scientific methods, the creativity mill alluded to previously.

Today astronomers have a hard time persuading many in the general public that the Universe is very old and very big, that it did not wait until we could take center stage -- that it had billions of things to do, over billions of years, before we showed up to declare how important we are.















Science is, and must be, culturally embedded; what else could the product of human passion be? . . . Culture is not the enemy of objectivity but a matrix that can either aid or retard advancing knowledge.  Stephen Jay Gould
 Have we not all wondered at some time or another where the human race has gotten all its ideas? I remember watching an aunt make wheat bread when I was a small child. I remember bothering her with question after question about what she was doing. "How is it made? What are the ingredients? Wheat? What is wheat? A plant? From the seed of a plant?" How could human beings ever figure out such a thing? How did we connect the seed of a common weed to make the bread for my peanut butter and jelly sandwich?

Jean-Paul Sartre, in his novel, Nausea, tells the story of a man who has decided, "Science! It is up to us." This man has decided to read every book in his city library so that he will know everything there is to know. He started with the first book on the first shelf, and by the time Sartre's hero Antoine Roquentin meets up with this "self-taught man" he is into the L's and reading Peat Mosses and Where to Find Them. According to Sartre,

    He has read everything; he has stored up in his head most of what anyone knows about parthenogenesis, and half the arguments against vivisection. There is a universe behind and before him. And the day is approaching when closing the last book on the last shelf...he will say to himself, "Now what?"
He will know many facts, but will he have made any connections? Will he be able to add to our knowledge of nature's secrets? During the third century B.C. in the city of Alexandria, Egypt, another admirer of books by the name of Eratosthenes was reading about a curious fact. Eratosthenes was the head librarian of the famous library of Alexandria. The knowledge stored in this library was far ahead of its time, and he had access to many great thoughts and facts. The fact that preoccupied him this day was rather ordinary on the surface. He read that in the city of Syene (the site of modern day Aswan), approximately 500 miles away, on the day of the summer solstice, June 21st, the longest day of the year, at exactly noon, the Sun shone directly over a particular water well casting no shadow. Only at this time of year for a brief moment could people see all the way to the water at the bottom of the well.

The average person would probably not even notice this. For the more thoughtful, it was at least curious. Someone had to notice the event and connect it with the summer solstice. Even the observation of a fact requires insight and creativity. For the brilliant Eratosthenes, however, nature was taunting us with one of its secrets. There was a very important connection.

It is doubtful that it happened this way, but for dramatic effect imagine that Eratosthenes first came across this little fact a few minutes before noon, June 21st. We can imagine him looking up from the book with a passionate startled look and staring blankly into space. He then rushes outside to find a tall, straight pillar and observes at exactly noon a shadow cast by the pole. He then jumps up and down in ecstasy at this great discovery: There was no shadow in Syene, but at exactly the same time there was still a shadow in Alexandria. What is the connection?

With a few reasonable inductive generalizations, and several valid deductions, Eratosthenes discovered not only that the Earth is spherical, but also that its circumference is very large -- approximately 25,000 miles. It was an ordinary fact, but a very big fact indeed.

He had a great deal of help in making this discovery. Scientific discoveries are made within a social context. Not only did he have help in obtaining all the facts -- someone needed to measure the distance from Alexandria to Syene and someone had to observe and note the connection of a shadowless well at Syene with the date and time -- but, more importantly, the intellectual environment which made Eratosthenes' conclusion possible owed its existence to the ancient Greek rationalists. The idea that a few facts could go a long way with a little reason was already part of the Greek heritage.

The city of Alexandria was founded by the Greek conqueror Alexander the Great. With him came the intellectual tradition of faith in reason, the passion to explore the physical and biological world of his tutor Aristotle, the belief in natural law of the pre-Socratics, and the triumph of the mathematical teachings of Pythagoras, Plato, and Euclid -- particularly geometry. It was geometry, and a belief, fostered by the rationalists, that with mathematics we possess a mystical power to transport our minds and "see" what our physical eyes may never see, that allowed Eratosthenes, to a large extent, to connect up these curious facts.

First of all, what could account for there being a shadow in one place, but not at another? One possibility was that the Sun was relatively close to the Earth. When the Sun was directly over the well in Syene, it would be at an angle in relation to the pole in Alexandria, thus casting a significant shadow. A more likely possibility based on very good inductive reasoning is that the Sun is very far away, far enough that by the time its light reached the Earth, the light rays would be virtually parallel to each other. The Sun heats the entire Earth, so it is likely to be very large. The Moon does not heat the Earth. Yet during an eclipse of the Sun by the Moon, the Moon fits perfectly over the Sun. If a quarter is held far enough away from your eyes it will appear the same size as a dime that is much closer. Thus, the large Sun is probably very far away to appear to be the same size as the Moon.

If the Sun is very far away, then one way to account for the shadow in one place, but not the other, would be to assume that the surface of the Earth is curved. And if it is curved, it was reasonable for Eratosthenes to conclude that its curved surface met to form a sphere, a round Earth. This was not a new idea. Pythagoras had argued as early as the fifth century B.C. that the sphere was the most perfect object for the Earth to be shaped, and Aristotle and others had noted that during the eclipse of the Moon, the dark shape made on its face was that of a disk. (They assumed that the eclipse was caused by the Earth's shadow moving across it.)

As the master keeper of this tradition, Eratosthenes knew that circular objects can be measured and analyzed using a method of proportion. (For the purpose of illustration we will assume he knew about degrees. Actually, he used fractions.) He also knew from the geometry of Euclid that the alternate angles formed by a line intersecting two parallel lines would be equal. Thus, he reasoned deductively, by measuring the angle of the shadow in Alexandria, we would automatically know the angle made by drawing a line from Alexandria to the center of the Earth and another line from Syene to the center of the Earth (Figure 2-1). This angle would then automatically tell us not only the proportion of space taken up by the angle compared to the entire 360 degree circumference, but also the proportion of space taken up by the distance of Alexandria to Syene compared to the circumference of the entire Earth. Using a simple sundial, he measured the angle to be 7 degrees, approximately 1/50th of a circle. Thus, if the distance from Alexandria to Syene was 1/50th of the circumference of the entire Earth, then the Earth must be approximately 25,000 miles in circumference (500 x 50 = 25,000). At the dawn of Western civilization our ancestors were already beginning to understand the magnitude of the Universe.

Note that the average person during Eratosthenes' time would have had a very difficult time believing that the Earth was this big. Although the observation of a lunar eclipse supplied empirical evidence supporting the belief in a spherical Earth, and it was reasonable to assume at this early date that the Sun was very far away, no one possessed the means to circumnavigate the globe in the third century B.C. There were no pictures from space showing a beautiful blue spherical ball. The factual evidence that we take for granted today for a large spherical Earth did not yet exist. At this time, whether one believed in the validity of Eratosthenes' picture of the Earth depended more on one's faith in the power of combining deductive and inductive reasoning with only a few facts.(9)

Today astronomers have a hard time persuading many in the general public that the Universe is very old and very big, that it did not wait until we could take center stage -- that it had billions of things to do, over billions of years, before we showed up to declare how important we are. Yet, using a selection of crucial facts, they construct just as Eratosthenes did trails of deductive and inductive reasoning that produce reliable conclusions.

Geometry and an intellectual tradition, curious facts noted by someone else, accident and serendipity,(10) all of these supplied the ingredients for Eratosthenes' creativity mill. The connections are easy to see after someone has made them. The number of potential pieces, however, is always infinite; you can possess all the facts in the world like Sartre's self-taught man and unless you can connect them, all that results is "Now what?"

Eratosthenes had many biases, but these biases helped him make connections. He accepted the rationalism of the ancient Greeks and the idea that mathematics granted us a special power to transport our understanding to invisible places. Today, scientists in many ways continue this tradition. As Morris Kline has noted in his book, Mathematics and the Search for Knowledge, "adorned with a few physical facts," we have pushed our understanding to unimaginable places, places we will undoubtedly never visit.

We have noted that science is based on the epistemological position of empiricism. No matter how much logical deduction and mathematical analysis is used, at some point the world must be observed for the confirmation of a belief. Historically, however, spurred on by the power of mathematics and the tendency to conclude that we know something even though complete empirical observations are not available, rationalism has played both a constructive and creative role in the development of science. Philosophers are often criticized for being too rationalistic, using speculative logic in creating ivory-tower armchair fantasies. This criticism, however, overlooks the fact that many great scientific discoveries are made by scientists sitting at desks, following the elegant trails of mathematical equations. Creative ideas are the result of a complex web of influences. The important thing is to have ideas with which to make connections.


Philosophical Issues
Great scientists have not been immune from detrimental cultural dogmas.

























An extremely healthy dose of skepticism about the reliability of science is an absolutely inevitable consequence of any scientific study of its track record.  Michael Scriven, Reasoning

Of course, not all ideas are fruitful in making connections. Nor have great scientists been immune from detrimental cultural dogmas. The famous sixteenth-century astronomer Tycho Brahe was the best observational astronomer of his time. The data he recorded on the motion of the planets were crucial to our modern understanding of the solar system. Mathematically, he knew that one of the implications of this data was that the Sun was the center of the motion of all the planets, which further implied that the Universe was very large, and that the stars were an immense distance away. He could not bring himself to accept this radical conclusion. Instead he accepted traditional view for his time that the Earth was the center of the Universe, because it was inconceivable that God would "waste" this much space! If human beings were God's special creatures, he would not make the universe so large as to make us insignificant.

Johannes Kepler, who with Tycho's data finally solved the problem of planetary motion using elliptical orbits, was motivated by his belief that the Sun was the most appropriate object to be placed in the center of the Universe because it was the material home or manifestation of God.  A very strange belief from our modern perspective. Galileo, in spite of his brilliant astronomical observations and terrestrial experiments, failed to see the importance of Kepler's solution of planetary motion because it did not involve using perfect circles for the motion of the planets.  He believed incorrectly that comets were mere atmospheric events and that the tides were caused (also incorrect) by the motion of the Earth.

Many modern physicists reject literal interpretations of mathematical equations that imply that an event could take place before its cause. Could a bomb explode before a signal is sent to detonate it? Is this inconceivable? Do we know that it is impossible for any human being to ever experience this? Do we know that it is impossible for any form of consciousness to experience it? How do we know this? If we believe we know this on the basis of experience, then the nature of inductive reasoning requires that we say it is unlikely that such events will ever happen. If we believe we know absolutely that such events cannot happen, we will be accepting the rationalist's basic claim that some things can be known without observational experience.

Must science assume some ideas dogmatically? Is science dogmatic in assuming a particular epistemology as absolute? Must science assume that it is the only avenue to truth? Are there better ways of knowing than that allowed by inductive reasoning? How can science validate its own methods?

The mystics claim there are some very simple acts of knowing that cannot be described by an objective language. For instance, how does one really know how to drive a car? Is it not an intuitive feeling that one just gets after some practice? Someone may help point the way with descriptions of what to do, but the final knowing cannot be described by any language. How about the experience of seeing a death on the highway? Does a cold scientific description, "the cause of the cessation of bodily function was due to a rapid deceleration," accurately convey the truth? What about our own deaths? That each of us will die someday is an objective fact. But there seems to be much more to this truth than can be described in the statement, "I am mortal." Are there subjective truths, truths that cannot be described in an objective language? Is science being dogmatic in excluding the possibility of subjective truths?  And what of our moral judgments?  Do we not use, and wisely so, subjective feelings to "know" that killing children is wrong?

. . . we are as capable of learning more about how to do science as we are of learning more about how the world works. Larry Laudan

Most scientists today accept an assumption that can be traced to the ancient Greeks: Whatever they are, the basic truths of the Universe are "laws" that do not change, only our ideas about them do. Scientific objectivity presupposes that there is one set of truths, and our personal beliefs or the beliefs of scientists of a particular time either match these truths or they do not. The Earth is spherical, not because scientists believe it is, but because it is one way for everyone whether they know this or not. In other words, most scientists assume that beliefs about what is real do not affect what is real. Truth results only when our beliefs about what is real correspond to what is real.

This assumption may not, however, be essential to science. In Chapter 8, Quantum Theory and Reality, we will see that some physicists have actually proposed that the points of view implied by our experiments can affect the nature of reality; that instead of assuming there is only one reality, and one set of true descriptions about it, these physicists have proposed that there can be "complementary" realities. And some reputable scientists have not only criticized the traditional scientific assumption, but also proposed that a person's state of mind may have a bearing on whether he or she is prone to diseases such as cancer and that cures and remissions may be possible using psychological therapy. The belief that there is only one reality can itself be subjected to scientific scrutiny. There could be multiple realities or perhaps no reality that is physical!

Even though scientists at any given time may be caught within a web of many assumptions, science at its best does not rely on many assumptions. As we have seen, science does have basic values. It values testability and critical evaluation, because thus far it appears that the more we think critically about our beliefs, the more likely we are to know the truth.









Philosophers believe abstract questions are important because they are intimately related to the concerns of who we are, where we have come from, and what may be in store for us in terms of the survival of our species on this fragile fragment of the universe.

Critics of science will argue that these are simply the values of a particular historical culture, and that critical thinking is not necessarily such a marvelous human characteristic. Critical thinking, they argue, makes life more complicated, uncovers distracting details, and makes it less likely that human beings will discover the simple solutions to life's problem. There are also nihilists who argue that our so-called intelligence and our ability to be aware of the details of the universe are an evolutionary dead end, that far from producing the good life, our awareness and rationality are the cause of our craziness.

This debate reveals that even the values of science are subject to rational debate. Defenders of science will argue that even if some assumptions are necessary in the application of scientific methods, these assumptions are validated by the track record of success. Critics will argue this justification is circular. It simply raises the problem of induction again. It is circular reasoning to attempt to vindicate inductive reasoning by asserting that so far inductive reasoning has worked, because this vindication itself is an inductive argument. The history of science shows there have been many beliefs that have been "successful" for a time, only later to be shown to fail. Recall that no matter how much evidence there is for an inductive conclusion, it is always logically possible for the conclusion to be false. Thus, it is logically possible for the scientific process to completely fail tomorrow, even if it is true that it has been successful for centuries. And if it is conceivable that it could fail tomorrow, the question remains whether it is reasonable to continue to believe in the scientific process as helpful for our future. Defenders of science will argue that even the values and methods of science could change if we discovered good reasons to change them.(11) We should not worry much about mere conceivable change. Change could well be a sign of growth and learning. Our methods and values could improve over time.

 
 Has science been successful? No other creature on Earth is potentially as violent as the human race. Potential nuclear and biological war, and environmental destruction are serious problems. No matter what one believes is responsible for this massive destructive potential, it is indisputable that the science has made the means possible.

The vantage point of philosophy makes discussion on all these topics possible. Philosophers believe these abstract questions are important because they are intimately related to the concerns of who we are, where we have come from, and what may be in store for us in terms of the survival of our species on this fragile fragment of the universe. It is important, however, to tie these discussions down with as much concrete science as possible. Thus, with a few terms and issues under our belt, let's continue our tour of the cosmological development that has made it possible for us to ask such questions, by discussing what scientists have discovered to be our biological roots.

Suggested Readings


Dismantling the Universe: the Nature of Scientific Discovery, by Richard, Morris (New York: Simon and Schuster, 1983).

A book intended for the informed general reader as well as scientists and philosophers. Includes a discussion of the critical evaluation process of science, plus the importance of creativity in scientific discovery. Also emphasizes the prominence of theories in observing the world, and the dismantling of the conceptual universes implied by our theories as a necessary condition for scientific revolutions.

How We Know: An Exploration of the Scientific Process, by Martin and Inge F. Goldstein (New York: Plenum Press, 1978).

The Goldsteins explain the scientific method and attitude through case studies with commentary. They discuss extended examples from biology (cholera), physics (heat and atomic theory), and psychology (schizophrenia), experimentation, measurement, the nature of theories, logic and mathematics, and probability and statistics.

The Rise of Scientific Philosophy, by Hans Reichenbach (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1951).

Written by an important philosopher of science, who studied with Einstein and made major contributions to the philosophical understanding of the theory of relativity, this book was one of the first attempts to reach a wider audience in explaining the importance of the philosophy of science.

Conjectures and Refutations; the Growth of Scientific Knowledge, by Karl Raimund Popper (New York: Harper & Row, 1965).

For anyone interested in the philosophy of science, this is must-read book by the most influential philosopher of science of the twentieth century. Emphasizing the significance of the refutability and vulnerability of scientific theories, Popper discusses induction and other matters related to the problem of scientific justification from a broad historical perspective.

The Limits of Science, by Nicholas Rescher (Berkeley: University of California Press, 1984).

Although the reader will need to labor through some professional terminology, once such convoluted locutions as "intrasystemic" explanation, "ideational" verses "pragmatic commensurability," and the principle of "question propagation" are understood, one will uncover a provocative reflection on many of the problems posed in our philosophical interlude. Why does each generation of scientists select a new version of what is reasonable from the conceivable? Because, according to Rescher, there is an infinite texture to nature that constantly eludes the grasp of finite human beings and their scientific explanations. Is this cause for despair? No, because the limit of science as being condemned forever to being an open-ended enterprise gives it an open-ended creativity to explore the infinite textures. What then justifies our relationship with nature as progressive? "Pragmatic commensurability," modern views are technologically superior to previous ones.

Science and Subjectivity, 2d ed., by Israel Scheffler (Indianapolis: Bobbs Merrill, 1982).

This book is concerned with the problem of objectivity in science. If inductive reasoning does not offer a means to separate the reasonable from the conceivable, and our theories inevitably distort observations and mask reality, how can science be said to be an objective enterprise? Scheffler attempts to answer this question and in the process provides a good discussion of the issues and the major philosophers of science.

The Structure of Science: Problems in the Logic of Scientific Explanation, 2d ed, by Ernest Nagel (Indianapolis: Hackett Pub. Co, 1979).

A popular upper division college textbook for introductory philosophy of science courses. Other good introductory textbooks include Philosophy of Science, by Arthur Coleman Danto and Sidney Morgenbesser (New York: Meridian Books, 1960), Philosophy of Science; the Link Between Science and Philosophy, by Philipp Frank (Englewood Cliffs, N.J.: Prentice-Hall, 1957), and The Anatomy of Inquiry; Philosophical Studies in the Theory of Science, by Israel Scheffler (New York: Knopf, 1963).

Science and Values: The Aims of Science and Their Role in Scientific Debate, by Larry Laudan, (Berkeley Calif.: University of California Press, 1984.

Laudan defends what he calls a reticulated model of scientific justification. Science is in the business of producing theories that are successful. The judgement of the success of those theories is based on methods. Methods in turn are instrumental means for fulfilling cognitive values (our goals and aims). The whole process is networked and subject to change and learning. New theories can cause us to change our values, as well as learn new methods. Nothing is absolute, but the whole process is rational and objective. Basically, because everything in science is fallible, we learn over time not only more about the world but also how to do better science.

The Philosophy of Science: A Contemporary Introduction (Routledge Contemporary Introductions to Philosophy), by Alexander Rosenberg (Routledge: 2000).

A user friendly introduction to the technical field of the philosophy of science.  Many introductions to the philosophy of science are very expensive.  In addition to covering the basics, Rosenberg's book is only about $30.
